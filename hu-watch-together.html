Why Fans Don't Want to Watch Alone Anymore

390+ events in, here's what we've learned about community, belonging, and why the big screen is always better with your people.

You can watch almost anything, almost anywhere, almost instantly. That was supposed to be the dream. And in a lot of ways it is — but it came with a quiet trade. When everything is available, nothing feels like an occasion.

We noticed something running screening after screening: people weren't turning up just to see a film. Most of them had already decided they'd watch it eventually anyway. They turned up to watch it together.

The streaming paradox

Solo viewing optimises for convenience. You pause, you scroll, you finish it across three nights. It's efficient — and efficiency is exactly what drains the magic out of a moment you've been waiting months for.

A packed room can't be paused. Everyone gasps at the same frame, laughs at the same beat, goes silent at the same turn. That shared timing is something a living room can't manufacture.

A cultural moment isn't something you press play on alone at 1 a.m.

What 390+ events taught us

Across 56,000+ fans in Mumbai, Bengaluru, Ahmedabad and Indore, the pattern held. The pre-show matters as much as the film. The crowd's energy is part of the product. People will travel, dress up, and pay more for a night that feels designed for them rather than processed through them.

So we design for the room, not just the screen — calibrated audio, immersive pre-shows, creator moments, games and giveaways. The film is the reason; the room is the experience.

Belonging is the real feature

Underneath all of it is something simple. Fandom is lonely when it's solitary and electric when it's shared. Put people who care about the same thing in the same room and you don't get an audience — you get a community that recognises itself.

That's why fans don't want to watch alone anymore. And it's why we'll keep building rooms worth showing up to.
